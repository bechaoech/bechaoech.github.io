Our Journey


We’re not the most outstanding students, nor are we anyone particularly special. But we did it — from two undergrads in Vietnam 🇻🇳 to now preparing to move to the U.S. 🇺🇸 together: one pursuing a PhD, the other walking alongside as a steady companion.

I’m writing this not only to capture a meaningful chapter of our lives, but also to share everything I once wished someone had told me from the very beginning. 📝

I hope our journey can offer encouragement to others who are also nurturing dreams of studying abroad — and growing together with someone they love. 🌱💖


About Us

As I shared in the About Me section, I studied Business Administration at the University of Economics Ho Chi Minh City (UEH), majoring in Quality Management. My husband, on the other hand, majored in Artificial Intelligence within the Computer Science program at the University of Information Technology (UIT), Ho Chi Minh City. We’re the same age, so our academic journeys were almost parallel—from freshman year all the way to graduation.

I met him during the second semester of our first year. Back then, we were just two freshmen still trying to figure things out—questions like “What will we do after graduation?” or “Where are we heading?” felt distant and unanswered. We didn’t have any clear plans for the future. We simply studied together, helped each other out, and tried our best to grow a little more each day.

My English was very weak at the time since I had no solid foundation, and the first few semesters were really challenging. He was the one who patiently taught me grammar, tenses, and sentence structures. In return, I helped him with general education courses—because he was so passionate about algorithms that he spent nearly all his time on them, often neglecting the other subjects. In that sense, we truly supported each other: he lifted me up in English, and I helped him keep up with the rest.

We also had a little “tradition” — we set academic goals for each other, both short-term and long-term. Whoever failed to meet their goal had to “pay the price”: go pick the other person up and treat them to a meal. It was a fun challenge, but in the end, it kept us both motivated to improve day by day.

As a result, we both graduated early and earned our degrees with distinction.


Background

About six months before graduation, my husband started his first job at VinAI as an AI Research Resident. It was during this time that the first thoughts about pursuing a PhD began to take shape. He realized that if he wanted to go further in the field of Artificial Intelligence, building a strong academic foundation would help him connect more deeply with experts in the field and open doors to meaningful involvement in cutting-edge projects at major tech companies.

At first, the idea of pursuing a PhD was just something he casually mentioned during one of our meals together. But gradually, I began to see a shift — the way he talked about it, the way he researched and started laying out a plan — it became clear to me that this was no longer just a fleeting thought.

Around a year and a half before the intended start of his PhD, he sat down with me to seriously discuss the idea of applying. And that’s when our real journey began.

What made me feel that putting my own career on hold to fully support him was the right decision, was when he seriously talked to his family about wanting to marry me, and requested that they hold an engagement ceremony so he could have the legal status to walk this next chapter of life with me — as husband and wife.

He also clearly told me:

“I will only apply to universities in countries where you can come with me. If you can’t, then I won’t go.”

There were no dramatic promises, no poetic “wait for me” speeches like in the movies — just a calm, clear conviction. And that was exactly what gave me full trust in him — not because of what he said, but because of what he did.

At the time, I felt we were still a bit young to have a wedding, so I suggested we just have a formal engagement and register our marriage first. I’ve always wanted us to save our wedding ceremony for when we return — more mature, more experienced — so we could celebrate it with full meaning and joy.

Once everything was in place, he resumed his research at VinAI. As for me, I chose not to work and devoted all my time and energy to supporting him in his endeavors.


Our Full Journey

Our journey began with the smallest of thoughts — when my husband first brought up the idea of pursuing a PhD at one of the world’s top universities. Back then, neither of us had a clear picture of what was to come: dozens of emails to professors, countless late nights refining his Statement of Purpose, and the anxious wait for each round of responses from schools. We didn’t have a roadmap or an overall plan — everything was completely new to us. But step by step, through endless research, learning, trial and error, and constantly encouraging each other, we slowly moved closer to something we once only dared to dream of: receiving an offer of admission, holding two visas in our hands, and getting ready to fly to the U.S. to begin an entirely new chapter of our lives. This post is a record of that journey — not just to preserve a meaningful memory for us, but also to serve as a practical guide for anyone who is taking their first steps toward applying for a PhD and preparing to study abroad alongside the one they love.

1. Timeline

Although we had serious conversations about pursuing a PhD more than a year before the actual start date, at that time we were still very unclear about how the application process worked — from writing the SOP, contacting professors, to preparing financial documents. Honestly, we had no concrete preparations in the early stages. After our engagement ceremony, he focused entirely on his research and writing papers for conferences, while I concentrated on improving my English. As a result, the whole PhD application process was… pretty much left untouched. It wasn’t until August 2024 that I finally opened the admissions websites of different universities for the first time and began learning everything from scratch.

2. Defining the Goal and Research Direction

"Why do you want to pursue a PhD?" — that was the very first question both of us had to seriously reflect on together.

At first, he simply said he wanted to do a PhD to gain the opportunity to work in a more research-focused environment. But gradually, after many conversations, he came to realize that his true motivation stemmed from a desire to become a researcher who could make long-term contributions — not just someone solving short-term problems in industry.

Another reason was his wish to build a strong academic foundation, enabling him to connect more effectively with the academic community and open doors to top research labs at major tech companies like Google, Meta, or NVIDIA... A PhD, he realized, would not only grant him academic credibility but also allow greater freedom in choosing research directions — something difficult to achieve in most engineering roles.

Once the “why” was clear, we moved on to the next big question:
“What do you want to research over the next 4–5 years?”

We looked back at his background. At the time, he already had some experience in AI and Computer Vision, especially from his research projects at VinAI. That made directions like 3D Vision, Multimodal Learning, and Robotics a logical and meaningful path to follow. We agreed that the goal would be to apply to professors and labs that specialize in Computer Vision and Robotics — places that could help him dive deeper into these fields and grow in line with his long-term vision.

Finally, after identifying both the purpose of pursuing a PhD and the research direction, we sat down to analyze his background using the SWOT framework (Strengths – Weaknesses – Opportunities – Threats). This helped us clearly see what strengths to leverage, what weaknesses to address, and what opportunities to focus our efforts on. That step turned out to be essential — it laid the foundation for writing a compelling Statement of Purpose later on.

SWOT Analysis▼

Strengths
  • Strong research background despite not holding a Master’s degree
  • Excellent academic record and GPA
  • Notable achievements at major conferences (e.g., CVPR, ICCV)
  • Clear and prominent research direction
  • Practical, real-world research experience with impactful applications
  • Strong Letters of Recommendation from reputable scholars
  • Well-rounded personal skillset
Weaknesses
  • No Master’s degree (applying directly from undergraduate)
  • No prior study or work experience abroad
  • Lack of internship experience at Big Tech companies or international startups
  • First-generation PhD applicant
  • Coming from a country with limited access to international academic opportunities.
  • Limited preparation time due to overlapping commitments (e.g., CVPR deadline and application timeline), leading to rushed IELTS and SOP development.
Opportunities
  • Rapid growth in 3D Vision, Robotics, and Multimodal LLMs
  • Full-time support from spouse throughout the entire application process
  • U.S. academic institutions are increasingly open to PhD applicants from Asia
  • Strong academic references, including professors already based in the U.S.
  • Targeted well-aligned research labs in 3D Vision and Robotics with strong track records in mentoring PhD students.
Threats
  • Large numbers of competitors from top-tier institutions (e.g., Berkeley, CMU, MIT)
  • Potential visa challenges related to F-1 and F-2 application
  • Some robotics labs emphasize physical/hardware robotics, which may not align with a perception-focused background
  • Applying during a period of significant funding cuts across U.S. academic institutions, reducing the number of available RA positions and increasing competition
