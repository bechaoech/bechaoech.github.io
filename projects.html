2. Defining the Goal and Research Direction

"Why do you want to pursue a PhD?" ‚Äî that was the very first question both of us had to seriously reflect on together.

At first, he simply said he wanted to do a PhD to gain the opportunity to work in a more research-focused environment. But gradually, after many conversations, he came to realize that his true motivation stemmed from a desire to become a researcher who could make long-term contributions ‚Äî not just someone solving short-term problems in industry.

Another reason was his wish to build a strong academic foundation, enabling him to connect more effectively with the academic community and open doors to top research labs at major tech companies like Google, Meta, or NVIDIA... A PhD, he realized, would not only grant him academic credibility but also allow greater freedom in choosing research directions ‚Äî something difficult to achieve in most engineering roles.

Once the ‚Äúwhy‚Äù was clear, we moved on to the next big question:
‚ÄúWhat do you want to research over the next 4‚Äì5 years?‚Äù

We looked back at his background. At the time, he already had some experience in AI and Computer Vision, especially from his research projects at VinAI. That made directions like 3D Vision, Multimodal Learning, and Robotics a logical and meaningful path to follow. We agreed that the goal would be to apply to professors and labs that specialize in Computer Vision and Robotics ‚Äî places that could help him dive deeper into these fields and grow in line with his long-term vision.

Finally, after identifying both the purpose of pursuing a PhD and the research direction, we sat down to analyze his background using the SWOT framework (Strengths ‚Äì Weaknesses ‚Äì Opportunities ‚Äì Threats). This helped us clearly see what strengths to leverage, what weaknesses to address, and what opportunities to focus our efforts on. That step turned out to be essential ‚Äî it laid the foundation for writing a compelling Statement of Purpose later on.

3. Selecting Schools and Finding the Right Professors

After defining our PhD goals and research direction, we moved on to the next stage: choosing schools, countries, and identifying professors to apply to. This was one of the most crucial steps ‚Äî because applying to top-ranked schools isn‚Äôt necessarily the best strategy. What truly matters is applying to places where there are professors whose research aligns with ours and who are actively looking for PhD students that year. We divided the tasks between us based on our strengths. I was in charge of researching the admissions requirements of each school and country ‚Äî including degree prerequisites, GPA, English proficiency (IELTS/TOEFL), GRE (if applicable), tuition fees, and funding policies. I also considered visa-related conditions, prioritizing countries where I could easily accompany him as a dependent. After narrowing down the options, we realized that the U.S. was the most suitable choice. The visa process was more favorable, the academic environment was strong, the schools had high rankings, and ‚Äî most importantly ‚Äî the post-graduation career opportunities were promising. Based on that, we decided to focus on schools ranked in the Top 20 U.S. institutions according to CSRankings (2019‚Äì2024).

I created a detailed Google Sheet to compile all relevant information: admissions requirements, tuition, English and GRE expectations, application deadlines, and fees. Meanwhile, he was responsible for finding suitable professors and labs. He kept track of important details such as the relevance of the research topics, whether the professor was actively recruiting, and signs that the lab was open to new students.

Here‚Äôs how we conducted the lab search:


1. Visited each department‚Äôs faculty directory (usually in Computer Science or Robotics), and cross-referenced with CSRankings.
2. Checked if the professor was currently accepting PhD students ‚Äî often noted on the professor‚Äôs website or social media (like LinkedIn, X).
3. Read lab descriptions and recent projects to see if they aligned with our interests.
4. Reviewed the most recent papers ‚Äî since labs often have multiple members working on different topics, we had to filter for the work most relevant to us.
5. Assessed the size of each lab by the number of current PhD students, and reviewed their profiles to see what kind of academic background they had before joining ‚Äî for comparison with our own.


If the lab seemed like a good fit, he would record it in the shared Google Sheet I had created ‚Äî including the professor‚Äôs name, lab name, website, research keywords, and status of email contact. That sheet helped us manage data from 20 different schools in an organized, stress-free way.

Although reading through countless lab websites and professor profiles was tiring at first, we gradually realized something important: applying to the right lab is more important than applying to many. So instead of casting a wide net, we focused only on the places that truly felt like a match ‚Äî where the professor was responsive, the research direction was clear, and the academic environment fit our long-term goals.

4. Preparing Academic Documents

In this phase, we prepared all the materials that are commonly required in most PhD applications, including: CV, SOP (Statement of Purpose), PS (Personal Statement), LORs (Letters of Recommendation), IELTS certificate, academic transcript, and diploma. This stage not only required precision and completeness, but also served as a crucial opportunity to showcase the applicant‚Äôs academic ability, research direction, and compatibility with the program.

We continued to divide our work clearly: he was responsible for writing the content, while I focused on strategy and overall structure ‚Äî from brainstorming, outlining, and providing writing direction, to proofreading and finalizing the documents.

At the time, he was in the final stretch of completing some unfinished papers, so he prioritized finalizing them to include in his application. However, for key documents like the CV, SOP, PS, and LORs, he still did the primary writing ‚Äî because only he truly understood his own strengths, academic goals, and how they aligned with specific professors and labs.

As for me, I handled the following:

  • Brainstormed and outlined the CV, SOP, PS, and each letter of recommendation
  • Drafted LORs tailored to each school and professor
  • Reached out to mentors early to ask for letters with ample time
  • After he completed the core writing, I took care of formatting, error-checking, and polishing each document before submission

CV (Curriculum Vitae)

We wrote all application documents in LaTeX to ensure professionalism, cleanliness, and consistency in formatting ‚Äî except for the Drafts LOR, which were written in Google Docs for easy back-and-forth feedback. The CV focused on academic components: research projects, publications, awards, and technical skills. I also studied many CV samples from current PhD students to better understand how to highlight strengths effectively. Sample CV

SOP (Statement of Purpose)

This was the hardest and most critical part of the application. We spent a huge amount of time writing, revising, discussing, and rewriting it again and again. A strong SOP needs to convey:

  • Long-term academic and research goals
  • Why you want to pursue a PhD and your specific research direction
  • How your personal experiences connect to your academic path
  • Why you chose this university and these specific professors/labs

In reality, the admissions committee doesn‚Äôt know you ‚Äî they‚Äôve never met, spoken to, or worked with you. The SOP is the first and only place where they can get to know you through your writing. But they also read hundreds of applications a day, so your SOP must be concise yet deep, clear but not boring, and sharply focused.

One thing we kept reminding ourselves ‚Äî and now I‚Äôll say it three times to anyone writing their SOP:

  • Do not use the same SOP for every school.
  • Do not use the same SOP for every school.
  • Do not use the same SOP for every school.

Each school and each lab has different expectations, styles, and research directions. That‚Äôs why customizing and tailoring each SOP is not optional ‚Äî even if it‚Äôs a lot of work, it‚Äôs absolutely worth it. Sample SOP

PS (Personal Statement)

Only a few schools required PS, as most only asked for an SOP. If a school did request a PS, we treated it as a space to share the personal side of the story ‚Äî the experiences that shaped the academic journey, personal growth over time, and values that aligned with a research environment.
I viewed the PS as a kind of emotional map that made the application less dry and more human. Do you remember the SWOT analysis we did in Section 2? We used our Strengths and Opportunities in the SOP, and our Weaknesses and Threats in the PS ‚Äî a perfectly complementary strategy.

Letters of Recommendation (LORs)

We reached out to our mentors quite early, but we only sent them the Drafts LOR after the SOP was fully completed. That‚Äôs because only after finalizing the SOP could I clearly see the overall narrative of his profile and determine what should be highlighted in the recommendation letters ‚Äî things that the applicant himself shouldn‚Äôt say directly, but could be confirmed by a trusted third party.

If the SOP is where the applicant speaks for themselves, then the LOR is where the mentor validates those claims. Most PhD applications require three letters of recommendation. So we prepared three separate drafts, each highlighting a different aspect of his strengths:

  • Writer X focused on research capability
  • Writer Y emphasized teamwork and proactivity
  • Writer Z highlighted persistence, resilience, and a growth mindset

This approach gave the admissions committee a more multidimensional and complete picture of the applicant, rather than reading three letters that repeat the same points.

What is an Draft LOR and Why Should You Prepare One?

Mentors ‚Äî whether they are professors, advisors, or supervisors ‚Äî are typically very busy people. They also work with many students and colleagues at the same time. Even if you‚Äôve worked closely with them on several projects in the past, over time, it‚Äôs natural that they may not remember all the details of what you did, how you performed, or what made you stand out. That‚Äôs where an Draft LOR comes in. It‚Äôs a summary of your contributions, milestones, and key qualities written by you, based on real experiences during your time working with the mentor. Preparing a draft doesn‚Äôt just save them time ‚Äî it also ensures the content is accurate, relevant, and well-aligned with your application goals, because you‚Äôve already gathered all the important information for them in advance.

After finishing the draft, I sent it along with clear notes, so that the mentor could easily revise it as they see fit, or simply approve and sign it if they agreed with the content.

Thanks to this level of preparation, the LOR process went smoothly for us ‚Äî the letters were personalized, targeted, and reflected the applicant‚Äôs actual strengths. Most importantly, we didn‚Äôt take up much of the mentors‚Äô time ‚Äî something we deeply appreciated. After all, just having them agree to write the letter is already a huge favor.

5. Submitting Applications and Tracking Progress

After completing all required documents, we moved on to the application submission phase ‚Äî which, although it might sound simple, turned out to be quite stressful. Each school has its own system, unique requirements, and small details that can easily be overlooked without a solid tracking strategy.

To stay organized, we created two main tracking systems:

1. Organizing Applications by School

We set up a main Google Drive folder called "Application Package", with a separate subfolder for each university ‚Äî such as CMU, UC Berkeley, UMD, etc. Inside each folder, we uploaded all submitted materials, including the school-specific versions of the CV, SOP, LORs, transcripts, degree certificates, and confirmation emails.

This system allowed us to quickly check what we had submitted to each school, especially helpful if any issues arose (e.g., a missing file or last-minute verification request).

2. Tracking LOR and Submission Status

To keep everything organized during the hectic submission period, I created a master Google Sheet to track the progress of all applications. I updated it daily to make sure no steps were missed.

In addition, we prepared individual tracking sheets for each recommender. These sheets made it easy for them to know which schools required letters, what the deadlines were, and whether the requests had been sent ‚Äî all without needing to check in with us repeatedly.

This approach helped minimize back-and-forth and reduced the burden on our recommenders, especially given the tight timeline and number of schools involved.

6. Cold Emailing Professors and Labs After Submission

After submitting all PhD applications, we began sending cold emails to professors whose labs my husband had applied to. While many applicants choose to reach out before submitting their applications, we intentionally waited until after submission ‚Äî so that professors could directly look up his name in the application system if they were interested.

We were very clear about our approach:

  • We only emailed professors whose research directions truly aligned with his, and only those listed in the submitted application forms (as most schools allow you to name 3‚Äì4 faculty members).
  • Each email was highly personalized, explaining specifically why his background and research goals matched the lab's direction. We absolutely avoided sending generic or copy-pasted messages
  • We sent emails within 1‚Äì2 weeks after submission, ensuring they wouldn‚Äôt arrive too late, while still being timely as professors began reviewing applications.

Each email included:

  • A brief self-introduction (academic background and recent research experience)
  • A mention that he had submitted his PhD application and expressed interest in joining the professor‚Äôs lab
  • A specific point of connection (e.g., a paper he had read or a project he was excited about)
  • Attached materials: CV + personal website link
  • A polite thank-you and an expression of interest in hearing back

He drafted each email himself, and I helped review and edit every line ‚Äî from grammar and phrasing to tone ‚Äî to make sure each message was concise, respectful, and thoughtful. There were times we spent over 30 minutes crafting just a few short sentences, simply because we wanted the email to be considerate of the professor‚Äôs time and attention.

Not every email received a response ‚Äî and we understood that. But a few professors did respond positively, confirming they had seen his application and would review it carefully. Some even mentioned that they would recommend his file to the admissions committee, or invited him to a lab interview shortly after reading his email.

So even though cold emailing after submission may seem late to some, when done correctly, it can still be a meaningful and strategic way to stand out, show genuine interest, and demonstrate your readiness to collaborate.

I remember it clearly: the first five schools that responded were all rejections. Each night, while we were working, the sound of a Gmail notification would make our hearts skip a beat. The moment we saw a school name appear in the inbox, our hearts raced ‚Äî only to open the message and find it began with the words, ‚ÄúWe regret to inform you‚Ä¶‚Äù Night after night, we checked our emails in silence, exchanged glances, sighed ‚Äî and then reminded each other:
‚ÄúIt‚Äôs okay. One right opportunity is enough.‚Äù Truthfully, we had mentally prepared ourselves for any outcome. So while the disappointment was real, we didn‚Äôt let it break us. I‚Äôve always believed that results don‚Äôt define our worth. And sometimes, closed doors exist only to make way for the right one to open at the right time.

And eventually, it did.

Around early February, my husband received his first offer ‚Äî from the University of Maryland, College Park (UMD). What made this case special was that we had only sent a cold email to the professor, who responded saying that my husband‚Äôs profile was a strong fit for the lab. A week later, he had a one-on-one interview with the professor, which lasted an hour. But things didn‚Äôt always go smoothly. We happened to apply during a period of political instability, and many schools were affected by funding cuts, which severely impacted several labs‚Äô admissions decisions. Even though the outcome wasn‚Äôt what we hoped for, we still felt seen and respected. It meant a lot to know that the effort we had poured into this process was truly recognized.

The Final Decision

In the end, we accepted the offer from University of Maryland, College Park (UMD) ‚Äî not because it was the most ‚Äúprestigious‚Äù school, but because it was the most suitable one for us. It was the place where the professor truly aligned with my husband‚Äôs research interests, where the lab focused on exactly the topics he was passionate about, where there was a strong academic community, and most importantly, where we could start this new chapter of our lives together. Choosing a school is a big decision, but we have no regrets ‚Äî because we had set our priorities clearly from the beginning, and we made our choice based on what mattered most to us, not to anyone else.

8. Preparing the F-1 and F-2 Visa Applications

After officially choosing a school and receiving the admission offer, we moved on to the next stage: applying for our U.S. visas. For us, this wasn‚Äôt just a personal administrative step ‚Äî it was a shared journey. My husband applied for an F-1 visa (international student), and I applied as his dependent under the F-2 visa category.

Step 1: Receiving the I-20 from the University

As soon as we confirmed enrollment with UMD, we followed the school‚Äôs instructions to request the Form I-20 for both of us:

  • His I-20 (F-1): For the PhD program
